# When to resist Awwwards-style

Awwwards rewards sites that make a strong statement. That's wonderful for the right project and wrong for the rest. This file is the decision guide.

---

## The core principle

Awwwards-style (high personality, custom cursors, heavy motion, dramatic typography, WebGL, scroll-synced reveals) **costs attention**. The user must stop, look, figure out. That cost is worth paying when the product **is** the experience. It's a tax when the user came for a transaction.

steve-designer's job in Phase 2 and 3 is to know which kind of project this is — before suggesting references.

---

## Pull toward Awwwards when...

### Portfolios and personal brands
The site *is* the product. Visual statement = credibility signal. Rauno, case studies, agency sites. Strong pull toward Awwwards trio.

### Product marketing for creative tools
Figma, Framer, Linear (for the marketing page, not the product UI). The visual craft signals the product's craft. Pull toward Awwwards-adjacent — but ground with editorial calm so users can still read.

### Brand and launch pages
Standalone campaigns, product launches, "coming soon" pages. Purpose is memorability, not conversion. Pull hard.

### Cultural, editorial, or entertainment projects
Magazines, films, music, art, museums. Readers expect to slow down. Awwwards style is often the native idiom.

### Hardware / physical products with aspirational positioning
Teenage Engineering, Arc Browser hardware pages, boutique watches. The web experience extends the object's presence.

---

## Resist Awwwards-style when...

### SaaS dashboards and admin UIs
The product UI is where users live 40 hours a week. Every decorative flourish is a paper cut. Pull from Linear (product side), Notion, Stripe Dashboard. Goal: disappear.

### E-commerce with conversion pressure
Shoppers decide in seconds. Custom cursors, scroll hijacking, and delayed reveals kill conversion. Pull from Shopify stores that work (Allbirds, Bellroy), Amazon's component discipline, Mercury-ish trust palettes. Motion only on confirmation/feedback.

### Financial products for mass consumers
Banking, taxes, retirement. Users already feel anxious. Flourish = perceived risk. Pull from Wise, Mercury, Ramp, but further toned down. High contrast, direct copy, zero WebGL.

### Healthcare, insurance, legal, government
Same logic as finance, amplified. Users need confidence they're in the right place. Pull from... honestly, the best government design systems (gov.uk, 18F). Any Awwwards reference will feel wrong.

### Accessibility-first products
Screen-reader users, motor-impaired users, cognitive-impairment users. Scroll hijacking, parallax, and custom cursors are hostile. Pull from WCAG-compliant design system sites.

### High-frequency productivity tools
Email clients, calendars, IDEs. Every pixel is familiar muscle memory. Changing layout for drama breaks workflow. Pull from Arc (application), Superhuman, Linear (product).

---

## The ambiguous middle

Not all projects are cleanly one or the other. The most common ambiguous cases:

### Developer tools (Linear, Vercel, Stripe's API docs)
**Verdict:** Pull from editorial-restraint references (Linear, Vercel) — they combine craft with utility. Don't pull from motion-forward Awwwards unless the team can hit 60fps and the audience appreciates it.

### B2B SaaS marketing sites
**Verdict:** Pull from Stripe, Mercury, Ramp — engineered warmth without flourish. Avoid pure Awwwards — B2B buyers conflate motion with immaturity.

### Consumer AI products
**Verdict:** Pull from Arc, Figma marketing, some Awwwards SOTDs — warmth and wonder matter. But the product UI itself should be calmer than the marketing.

### Agency and studio sites
**Verdict:** Almost always Awwwards-forward. Your visual statement is your pitch.

### Ecommerce boutique / luxury goods
**Verdict:** Pull from Aesop (restraint), selectively from Awwwards (artful product pages), avoid motion-for-motion's-sake. Luxury hates trying too hard.

---

## How to communicate the resistance

When the user says "I love [Awwwards SOTD] — make something like that" but their project is a fintech dashboard:

Don't just refuse. Reframe:

> "That site is doing a specific thing — it wants you to slow down and explore. Your users need to find their transaction history in three seconds. We can steal some of what makes that reference feel crafted — the typography discipline, the palette restraint, the specific motion timing — without stealing the behavior that works against your users. Want me to show you what 'inspired by X, but built for your context' looks like?"

Then pull two editorial-restraint references + one accent from the user's reference. That composition is almost always right for ambiguous projects.

---

## What happens when steve-designer ignores this file

Projects that mismatch their references look:
- **Wrong awards entries on productivity tools** → "beautiful but unusable"
- **Too polished on authentic brands** → "corporate, soulless"
- **Too motion-heavy on accessibility-sensitive contexts** → "inaccessible"
- **Too restrained on brand pages** → "forgettable"

The matching is the craft. Steve-designer's value comes from making it deliberate, not from maximizing either pole.
